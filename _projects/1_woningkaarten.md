More than thirty years ago, Charles Tilly challenged urban historians to tackle big questions about how large-scale social processes connect with local life. Yet the labor-intensive nature of archival research has limited historians' ability to answer these questions systematically. Recent advances in digitization and Visual Large Language Models (VLLMs) now make it possible to realize Tilly's vision at scale.

This project focuses on Amsterdam's woningkaarten (housing cards), maintained by the City Archives from 1924 to 1989. These roughly 400,000 handwritten cards systematically documented every residential address in Amsterdam, recording occupants' names, arrival and departure dates, addresses, and household composition. Spanning nearly seven decades of wartime occupation, postwar housing crises, migration, urban renewal, and deindustrialization, they form an extraordinary longitudinal record of urban life that has remained largely inaccessible to quantitative analysis.

We demonstrate how VLLMs can extract structured information from these handwritten records, transforming static documents into a dynamic, queryable dataset. Our processing pipeline interprets semi-structured layouts and links data across cards to reconstruct residential histories, track individuals and families through multiple addresses, and map neighborhood demographic change over time. This enables us to trace residential mobility patterns across social groups, examine how major historical events manifested in specific streets and buildings, and connect individual life courses with urban planning decisions.

The project also confronts the ethical dimensions of datafying personal information from administrative records, addressing questions about privacy, consent, and digital historians' responsibilities when working with sensitive data about real individuals and families. By making these housing cards computationally accessible, we provide both answers to specific historical questions about Amsterdam's development and a methodological model for similar projects elsewhere.
